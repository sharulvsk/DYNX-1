Cosmic Q&A

1. What is the cosmic background radiation?

Cosmic background radiation, also known as cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation, is the thermal radiation filling the universe, a remnant from the Big Bang. It is the afterglow of the initial explosion that created the universe and is observed as a faint glow in the microwave part of the electromagnetic spectrum.

2. What causes the colors of stars to vary?

The colors of stars vary due to their temperatures. Hotter stars emit more blue and white light, while cooler stars emit red and orange light. This variation is due to blackbody radiation, where the color of the light emitted by a star shifts based on its surface temperature.

3. What is a black hole, and how is it formed?

A black hole is a region in space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it. It is formed when a massive star collapses under its own gravity after exhausting its nuclear fuel. The core contracts to an extremely small volume, creating a singularity surrounded by an event horizon.

4. What is the difference between a galaxy and a nebula?

A galaxy is a vast system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound together by gravity, such as the Milky Way. A nebula is a large cloud of gas and dust within a galaxy. Nebulae can be sites of star formation or the remnants of dead or dying stars.

5. How do astronomers measure the distance to stars and galaxies?

Astronomers use several methods to measure distances in space. For relatively nearby stars, they use parallax, the apparent shift in position against the background stars as Earth orbits the Sun. For more distant objects, they use standard candles like Cepheid variables or Type Ia supernovae and redshift measurements from the expansion of the universe.
